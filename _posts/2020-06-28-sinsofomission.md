If you read my <a href="https://aziff.github.io/2020/06/22/helloworld.html">first blog post</a>, you already know that I strive to learn without subscribing to methodological ideology. How do the practical constraints and incentive structures of the academic track allow for such open learning? Akerlof (2020) discusses a pervasive ideology: (young) economists are pushed to produce research that is "hard" rather than "soft," at the expense of producing research that is important. Aggregating over the whole profession, the outsize weight on hardness over importance produces **"sins of omission,"** in which important questions are ignored. 

This paper astutely breaks down some reasons for hardness bias, a prominant element in the culture of economics, and the consequences of omitting important research questions.

Akerlof proposes three explanations for why economists emphasize hardness over importance.
1. **Economist self-image.** "Economics must be 'harder,' and therefore more scientific and precise, than other social sciences."
2. **Incentives.** Career advancement depends on journal acceptances decided by committee, and hardness is easier to agree on than importance.
3. **Selection.** Quantitative skills are rewarded, students with more quantiative skills enter the profession, quantitative skills are further rewarded, and a feedback loop ensues.

This hardness bias and the ensuing sins of omission have scientific and professional consequences. Not only are important questions overlooked; Akerlof explains how hardness bias contributes to the emphasis of publishing in "Top-5" journals and the protracted publishing process. 

<h3>Practical Steps for Open Learning</h3>

Akerlof offers recommendations for editors and departments, but what actions can graduate students take to combat hardness bias? To minimize my own hardness bias, I aim to:

* **Attend (virtual) seminars in different fields.**
* **Read and appreciate classic papers.** These can be "softer" than modern papers as Akerlof explains. With less precedent, comes less formal structure and precison.
* **Be a respectful referee.** If asked to be a referee, avoid making extraneous demands. Akerlof encourages referees to respect the authors and support the work rather than "holding them hostage."

Open learning also involves actively questioning priors and immediate reactions to research. It is easy to repeat disparaging remarks about "softer" approaches, fields, or disciplines. To avoid adopting these attitudes, I pose some questions for myself that might resonate with other graduate students.

* Do I fully understand the authors's assumptions and decisions? 
* Where _exactly_ do I start to question the authors's argument?
* Do my critiques have feasible solutions within the constraints of the work? 
* How do I view research in other disciplines, especially in other social sciences? 
* Do I automatically discount the conclusions of more qualitative methods? 
* Do I understand the guiding principles behind more qualitative methods?

These questions suggest humility as a central component of open learning, and perhaps a part of the solution to hardness bias more generally.
