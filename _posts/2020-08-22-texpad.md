_Economists, especially empiricists, rely on a handful of essential applications to produce research. This post is the first in a series in which I will discuss my favorite applications._ Most everyone in economics, whether an undergraduate student or a tenured professor, whether a theorist or an empiricist, whether a lecturer or a researcher, uses LaTeX.

As an Apple user, TexShop has been my historical go-to LaTeX editor. TeXShop is a great, FREE, editor. Its minimalistic design leads to smooth compilation with minimal glitches. However, for complicated documents with multiple subfiles, it can feel overwhelming to have more than one editing window open at once. Also, it does not have many interactive features to connect the PDF viewer to the text editor. 

A few months ago, I decided to research the alternatives available for Apple. Prioritizing clean interface, subfile organization, and helpful PDF viewing, Texpad emerged as an exceptional application. It costs $30, with the option for a 30-day free trial.

<img src="../../../../assets/img/texpaddemo.jpg" width="1000000">

These are the Texpad features that have been most useful to me.
* *Subfile organization.* I like being able to seamlessly switch between the various files that comprise a document, such as the preamble, BibTeX file, or appendix.
* *Navigation of the internal file structure.* Sections, subsections, and paragraphs are listed in a clickable menu for easy navigation to different parts of the file.
* *Auto-completion of citations and references.* Gone are the frustrating minutes spent searching for an in-text reference.

Texpad allows me to internally organize my files, reducing stress and errors as drafts develop and complicate. Beginners and experts alike will find much to enjoy in this application.
